Justice Principals

Below are 10 principals according to the Design Justice Network with some direct connections to topics discussed later.

  • Principal 1

    We use design to sustain, heal, and empower our communities, as well as to seek liberation from exploitative and oppressive systems.

  • Principal 2

    We center the voices of those who are directly impacted by the outcomes of the design process.

  • Principal 3

    We prioritize design’s impact on the community over the intentions of the designer.

  • Principal 4

    We view change as emergent from an accountable, accessible, and collaborative process, rather than as a point at the end of a process.

  • Principal 5

    We see the role of the designer as a facilitator rather than an expert.

  • Principal 6

    We believe that everyone is an expert based on their own lived experience, and that we all have unique and brilliant contributions to bring to a design process.

  • Principal 7

    We share design knowledge and tools with our communities.

  • Principal 8

    We work towards sustainable, community-led and -controlled outcomes.

  • Principal 9

    We work towards non-exploitative solutions that reconnect us to the earth and to each other.

  • Principal 10

    Before seeking new design solutions, we look for what is already working at the community level. We honor and uplift traditional, indigenous, and local knowledge and practices

Transparency and its Pitfalls

Explain how/why FAT is currently being used

Here are some examples of the pitfalls of transparency:

Transparency can be harmful

While transparency can be a means of exposing the wrongdoings of companies, it can at times come at the cost of vulnerable groups who depend on privacy to protect “counter hegemonic ideas and strategies”. The comparison has been made between the modern transcripts and those those of the organizers of the underground railroad during US slavery. Revealing these through “radical transparency” would have similarly dramatic impacts on these vulnerable populations.

Transparency can reveal too much information to be easily understood

Transparency seems ideal if one assumes that they will only be given the information on importance, but this is not always the case. Companies can use what is called resistant transparency to reveal a surplus of information to conceal the actually important information.

Transparency suggests that seeing is more important than understanding

Like with the garbage data dump that you saw at the beginning of this section seeing what is being collected was likely not that much insight into what this website had the potential to do with your data. In order for one to have a deep understanding of a system and its effects it is necessary to physically interact with it and see how changes made on the system change its outputs. Understanding how a system interacts with our data and its components is how a system can truly be monitored and regulated. If we can only see the system and our data that deep understanding of the system will be missing.

Transparency assumes a technical understanding and means of discussing what they are seeing

Just like when you were given the dump of raw data at the beginning of this section the only way you could have used this data was if you first had a base understanding of how digital data collection is constructed. Beyond this transparency is also only meaningful if you have a platform to discuss what you are seeing with other individuals. Systems that have both of these conditions are impossible to find.

An Issue of Problem Space
The problem space in which a piece of technology is designed can lead to incredibly biased systems regardless of how “unbiased” artificial intelligence can seem to be. In Indiana an automated welfare system was implemented and within 2 years there was a 12.2% increase in incorrect denial of food stamps for those who were applying for assistance. It is not surprising to find that the automation program was designed to reduce “fraudulent” welfare claims by replacing human case workers with AI systems. The intention of this system was to reduce welfare claims, and that came at the cost of those who needed governmental assistance most. With this new system clients no longer had a personal connection with their caseworkers and struggled to connect to people who could help them get back on welfare because of how depersonalized the system became. Simply because it was a computer booting people off of their welfare, does not make the system fair or equitable.
What is the Data Actually Represeting?
The claim has been made historically that data and the AI tools trained on that data are unbiased because they are designed to require minimum human input and make decisions based on algorithmic training. The issue is that areas that have been historically over policed due to structural injustices in policing methods will be overrepresented in the data sets being used to train these models. This will cause these policing prediction models to disproportionately predict crime in these same over policed areas, reinforcing the structures of inequality that it was “fixing”.
Understanding and Use of Results
AI systems cannot be the deemed ethical third parties because their results are only as unbiased as the people interpreting those results. Historically with risk assessment models, the results have been misinterpreted or misused to justify unjust sentencing for criminal offences. An example of this is when judges use scores that are intended to inform probation and instead using them to determine sentencing. Additionally it is worth mentioning that this is a very obvious example of how limited problem formulation can lead to a perpetuation of the existing problem. With this solution of using technology to replace the judge in determining bail instead of considering eliminating the cash bail system all together.
